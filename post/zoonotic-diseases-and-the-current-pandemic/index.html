Zoonotic Diseases and the Current Pandemic

March 25, 2020
pandemic climate change agriculture development

Reading the Trovao and Nelson papers about the origin and spread of pandemic influenza strains whilst experiencing it firsthand (in the ‘comfort’ of my own home) was quite surreal; it’s rare to read an academic paper that has tangible influence over ones person life. I would like to discuss how developing countries can minimize the probability of incidence of zoonotic diseases. The Trovao paper discussed how modern swine agriculture combined with global trade make the associated influenza virus extremely difficult to deal with. There is strong evidence that the Covid-19 virus emerged from a meat market in Wuhan, China, where there is regular contact between humans and many species of animals [1].

This is a strong argument against animal agriculture, compounded by the fact that the 2002 SARS virus also emerged from a meat market in Asia. Given the largest challenge of the century – and perhaps the human race – is climate change, it stands that many developing countries looking to improve the nutritional achievements of their citizens should look towards plant-based alternatives to animal protein. Doing so would drastically reduce the carbon emissions from agriculture, and there would be more land available for farming diverse crops. Global agricultural practices release 1⁄4 of the global greenhouse gas emissions [2].

The carbon budget for nations can be greatly improved by lowering rates of animal agriculture, thereby also promoting climate resilience, improved public health by reducing non-communicable diseases, and lowering the risk of zootosis. For example, the 2019 outbreak of e.coli spread by romaine lettuce was a result of the lettuce coming into contact with contaminated animal feces, thereby rendering a lot of food inedible [3].

There are many reasons why countries should focus on reducing animal agriculture, one of the main ones is that it is easy to get large amounts of people to change their eating habits, government propaganda works very well as evidenced by the dairy industry subsidizes and the Got Milk? advertising campaign. In conclusion, developing nations can reduce the risk of zootonic diseases, improve health, and develop climate resiliency by reducing animal agriculture, as it is one of the easier carbon sinks to get out of.
